The Answer:

Your understanding of the input/output translation process is 100% correct. You have perfectly mapped out the software layer's responsibility when parsing strings into stored bits and vice versa.

What you are proposing—using the MSB exclusively for the sign and leaving the remaining bits as the absolute value—is a real concept called Sign-Magnitude Representation. In this system, 19 is 00010011 and -19 is 10010011.

It seems completely logical from a storage and I/O perspective. So why does the system waste CPU cycles calculating 2's complement during read/print operations?

Because the system architecture is optimizing for the ALU (Arithmetic Logic Unit), not the I/O interface. Input/output operations happen occasionally; mathematical operations happen billions of times per second.

Here is exactly why 2's complement is the undisputed standard, viewed through the lens of hardware efficiency:

1. The Hardware Branching Problem
If you store numbers using your proposed Sign-Magnitude system, how does the CPU calculate A + B? The ALU's algorithm would look like this:

    Check the MSB of A and B.

    If signs match: Add the magnitudes together. Keep the sign.

    If signs differ: Compare the magnitudes to see which is strictly larger. Subtract the smaller magnitude from the larger magnitude. Attach the sign of the larger magnitude to the result.

At the hardware level, this is a nightmare. It requires an Adder circuit, a separate Subtractor circuit, magnitude comparators, and complex routing logic. This hardware branching slows down the fundamental clock cycle of the CPU.

2. The 2's Complement Exploit
With 2's complement, the ALU can be incredibly "dumb". It doesn't need to check the MSB. It doesn't care if the numbers are positive, negative, or mixed. It just throws the bits into a standard binary adder and ignores any overflow.

Let's look at 19 + (-5):

    19 in 8-bit: 00010011

    -5 in 8-bit 2's complement: 11111011

If we just perform a raw, blind binary addition:
00010011 (19)

    11111011 (-5)

100001110 <- Notice the 9th bit (the 1 on the far left).

Because we are in an 8-bit system, that 9th bit simply falls off the end of the register and vanishes. We are left with 00001110. Convert 00001110 to decimal, and you get exactly 14.

By taking the processing hit at the I/O bottleneck (converting the numbers to 2's complement twice), we allow the CPU to perform O(1) hardware addition without any conditional branching, whether it's adding or subtracting. Subtraction A - B just becomes A + (~B + 1).
